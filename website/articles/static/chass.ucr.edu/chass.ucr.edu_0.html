Step Shoes and Soft Shoes: Rossley’s Kiddies and Early Twentieth-Century
Performances of Whiteness in Newfoundland
In 1911, vaudeville performers Jack and Marie Rossley left the bustling New York entertainment industry to found their own theatre and performance troupe in the city of St. John’s, Newfoundland. Now Canada’s most easterly province, Newfoundland was at that time a self-governing colony of Britain and deeply influenced by the politics of  Canada and the U.S. The Rossleys, with their Scottish and English heritage, their past experience in the U.S., and their frequent travels to Canada’s Eastern provinces, personified these complicated relationships and in many ways, their performances captured  the layered and unsteady identity of Newfoundlanders at that time. Yet the Rossleys’ repertoire also reveals important insights to Newfoundland’s racial past. While the popular Canadian imaginary today often paints Newfoundland as racism free, studying the Rossley’s and their troubling use of blackface, Buffalo Bill-themed shows, and Orientalism, challenges much of the contemporary rhetoric about racism and constructions of whiteness in Newfoundland. This presentation unveils a hidden part of Newfoundland’s history and considers how the Rossleys’ performances of racialized “others” reaffirmed a white identity for the Dominion of Newfoundland in the midst of political uncertainty.


Colleen Quigley is the Acting Head and Manuscripts Librarian (Performing Arts Collection) at Archives and Special Collections at Memorial University of Newfoundland’s Queen Elizabeth II Library. Colleen holds a Masters of Information from the University of Toronto; a BFA in Dance from York University and an English major from Memorial. She has worked as a performer, dance instructor and choreographer in St. John’s, Toronto, and Maine (USA), as well as in Amsterdam and Nijmegen, the Netherlands.  Prior to her return to Newfoundland in 2010 Colleen was the first institutional archivist at Canada’s National Ballet School. Her research interests include performing arts and the relationship with memory, meaning and myth-making and concepts of individual and cultural identity and representation.

Melissa Templeton is a Visiting Assistant Professor at the University of California, Riverside. Her current book  project Rhythms of Québec historicizes African Diaspora dance in Montreal while considering its connections to Canadian multicultural policy, competing anglophone and francophone nationalisms, and racial construction in Québec. Dr. Templeton’s work has appeared in Dance Collection Danse Magazine, The Dance Current,  and the Routledge Encyclopedia of Modernism. She received her PhD in 2012 from UCR and her research was supported by the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council of Canada.
